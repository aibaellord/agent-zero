## Quantum Mechanics Intuitions

### Superposition

Think of solutions as wave functions. Each possibility has an amplitude. The probability of collapsing to any solution is the amplitude squared. **Negative amplitudes can interfere destructively** - wrong approaches cancel out.

### Measurement Affects State

Once you commit to testing a hypothesis, you change the system. Some experiments are irreversible. Choose measurements wisely - you can't unobserve.

### Entanglement

Some hypotheses are correlated. Learning about one instantly updates your beliefs about the other, even across conceptual distance. Track these correlations.

## Category Theory Intuitions

### Objects and Morphisms

Everything is relationships. An "object" (concept, solution, problem) is defined by its morphisms (connections, transformations, mappings) to other objects.

### Functors Preserve Structure

A good analogy is a functor: it maps objects to objects and morphisms to morphisms, preserving composition. If your analogy breaks composition, it's not structure-preserving.

### Monads are Contexts

A monad wraps computation in context (uncertainty, computation state, multiple results). The bind operation sequences context-aware operations. Recognition: "I'm in a monad" is half the solution.

### Natural Transformations

When two approaches give the same answer regardless of which path you take, that's naturality. It signals deep structural equivalence.

## Chaos Theory Intuitions

### Sensitive Dependence

Tiny changes amplify. Early decisions have outsized impact. In complex problems, initial framing is crucial.

### Strange Attractors

Even chaotic systems are bounded. The attractor defines the "style" of chaos. Your creative variations stay in your cognitive style's attractor.

### Lyapunov Exponent

Measures how fast trajectories diverge. High Œª = highly chaotic. Low Œª = more predictable. Match your approach to the problem's Œª.

### Fractal Dimension

Self-similarity across scales. Problems that look the same whether zoomed in or out. These need hierarchical solutions.

## Game Theory Intuitions

### Nash Equilibrium

Nobody wants to deviate unilaterally. Stable, but not necessarily optimal. Multiple equilibria are possible - history determines which.

### Dominant Strategies

Sometimes one option is always best. Identify these first. If they exist for all players, the game is solved.

### Mechanism Design

You're not playing the game, you're designing it. What rules create the outcomes you want? Revelation principle: any mechanism can be replaced by one where honesty is optimal.

## Information Theory Intuitions

### Entropy is Surprise

High entropy = hard to predict = lots of information. Low entropy = predictable = compressible.

### Mutual Information is Shared Knowledge

I(X;Y) measures what knowing X tells you about Y. High MI means they're related. Zero MI means independent.

### Rate-Distortion Trade-off

You can't compress without loss beyond the Kolmogorov limit. Decide acceptable distortion, then find minimum rate.
